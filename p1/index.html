Biographical Note:

Born in Burbank, California, I have lived in the greater Boston area ever since my undergraduate days at M.I.T. In 1976, I joined Wally Gilbert's lab in the Biological Labs on Divinity Ave (entrance flanked by two bronze rhinos), where I was an early user of the method for which he won a Nobel Prize ("Maxam-Gilbert" DNA sequencing). I joined the Cambridge-based biotechnology company Biogen in 1983, and for nearly thirty years oversaw a core facility that sequenced virtually every plasmid construct assembled there, reporting, curating, and storing the results in searchable form.

Since sequence data is represented as unwieldy strings of G, A, T, and C characters, essentially nothing can be readily inferred from it without the aid of computers. Fortunately, as sequencing technologies evolved, so did affordable computing. In the Biolabs, we started with a machine running on an 8 bit Intel 8080 chip with dual 8" floppy drives running code written in BASIC. At Biogen, I developed a client-server application written in 4D to interact with my clients, to drive laboratory automation, and to procure and analyze data. In 2001 I added a read-only web site using semi-dynamic pages for data delivery hosted by the 4D server. Later we built a web services portal (using SOAP) to access analytical capability on a remote unix box from the local 4D GUI. I have been intrigued by the wide use of PHP and MySql and intend to replace the client-facing functionality formerly provided by the 4D app with solutions based on these or similar industry-standard technologies.

I currently live in a rural corner of Framingham with my wife (Donna, a veterinarian), two kids (Emily, 18, and Henry, 16), and two ragdoll cats.
